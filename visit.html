Ladybower Reservoir


Ladybower Reservoir is a large, man-made reservoir located in the Upper Derwent Valley of Derbyshire, England. It is one of three reservoirs in the area, the other two being Howden Reservoir and Derwent Reservoir, collectively known as the Derwent Valley Reservoirs. Ladybower was completed in 1945 and is a key component of the Derwent Valley Aqueduct system, supplying water to the cities of Derby, Leicester, Nottingham, and Sheffield.

Ladybower was constructed between 1935 and 1943, and its construction involved the flooding of two villages, Ashopton and Derwent, which were subsequently submerged beneath the reservoir's waters. The project was a significant engineering undertaking and played a crucial role during World War II, when the reservoir was used by the Royal Air Force (RAF) for practicing low-level flying. The skills developed here were later employed in missions such as the famous Dam Busters raid in 1943.

Ladybower Reservoir has become a popular destination for tourists and locals alike. The surrounding area offers picturesque views and opportunities for outdoor activities, including walking, cycling, and birdwatching. There are several trails around Ladybower Reservoir, providing visitors with the chance to explore the beautiful landscape.

The reservoir and its surroundings provide a habitat for various wildlife species, including waterfowl, birds of prey, and fish. Fishing is permitted in designated areas of the reservoir, and it is a popular activity. Anglers can find a variety of fish species in the waters, including trout and perch.

Jacob's Ladder


Jacob's Ladder is a well-known and distinctive feature located in the Peak District National Park in England. It is a steep and challenging ascent, primarily used by hikers and outdoor enthusiasts. The ascent begins near the Kinder Reservoir and steadily climbs towards the Kinder Plateau. The landscape around Jacob's Ladder is rugged and offers panoramic views of the surrounding hills and valleys.

This particular trail poses various challenges and should not be approached without causion and preparation. Jacob's Ladder is renowned for its steep and challenging gradient. Hikers need to navigate a series of stone steps, which can be physically demanding, especially for those not accustomed to steep ascents. Furthermore, the weather in the Peak District can be unpredictable, with rapid changes in conditions. Rain and mist can make the path slippery, and the exposed nature of the route means that hikers can be faced with strong winds.

The history of Jacob's Ladder is closely tied to the broader historical context of access rights in the British countryside. Kinder Scout, where Jacob's Ladder is located, gained notoriety in 1932 when it became the focal point of the Kinder Scout Mass Trespass. This event was a protest by ramblers and working-class activists demanding the right to access uncultivated land for recreational purposes. The trespass played a crucial role in the establishment of national parks and the opening up of the countryside for public access. Today, thanks to the efforts of those trespassers, Jacob's Ladder and the surrounding paths are popular among hikers, walkers, and outdoor enthusiasts, attracting those seeking both a physical challenge and the opportunity to explore the scenic beauty of the Peak District.

B29 "Overexposed" Crash Site


The B29 crash site contains the remnants of a United States Army Air Forces Boeing B-29 Superfortress bomber that crashed on the moors near Bleaklow in 1948. The crash resulted in the loss of all 13 crew members. The causes are attributed to challenging weather conditions, including low visibility and icing, lead to navigational challenges. This historical site has garnered interest among hikers and aviation enthusiasts due to its unique blend of history and natural surroundings.

The B29 crash site is situated on the high moorland of Bleaklow, near the Snake Pass. Accessing the site requires moderate hiking experience. The most common approach is from the Snake Pass road, where you will find a marked trail leading you there. Hikers should be prepared for challenging terrain, including peat bogs and uneven ground.

While the crash site holds historical significance, it is important for visitors to exercise sensitivity and caution. The wreckage remains as a memorial to the crew members, and any exploration should be approached with a respectful and responsible mindset.

Mam Tor


Mam Tor, often referred to as the "Shivering Mountain" due to its unstable shale and rock layers, is an iconic hill in the Peak District National Park, England. Its history dates back thousands of years, with evidence of prehistoric settlements on its slopes. The hill is home to an Iron Age hillfort, believed to have been constructed around 1,200 BCE.

In the 20th century, Mam Tor gained notoriety for its geological instability. Landslips caused by the underlying shale layers led to the closure of the road that once ran across its flank, connecting the villages of Castleton and Edale.

Mam Tor is easily accessible and has become a popular destination for hikers and nature enthusiasts. There are several walking routes leading to the summit, providing varying degrees of difficulty to cater to different preferences and fitness levels. One of the most common routes begins at Mam Nick, a pass between Mam Tor and Rushup Edge. From Mam Nick, a well-maintained footpath leads to the summit. The ascent offers stunning panoramic views of the surrounding landscape, including the Hope Valley and Kinder Scout.
